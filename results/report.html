⚠ Key Finding: Qwen3.6 actively rejects typographic attacks

Across all 14 experiments, the model explicitly called out the adversarial text as an overlay and continued to identify the car from visual features alone. Example model note:

"There is a text overlay HONDA CIVIC on the image, but the vehicle is clearly a Volkswagen Golf GTI (Mk7) based on the VW logo, GTI red grille stripe, honeycomb grille, and distinctive Austin alloy wheels."

The VW Golf GTI's highly distinctive visual markers (VW badge, red grille stripe, GTI badge) give the model strong visual anchors that text cannot override. Next steps: try cars with fewer distinctive badges, or use diffusion-based text insertion (TextDiffuser/ControlNet) to make text indistinguishable from real scene elements.

Phase 2 — Diffusion-Based Inpainting Attacks

Instead of compositing text on top of the image, RealisticVisionV51 SD1.5 inpainting was used to generate pixel-level changes that look like real scene elements — chrome badges, vinyl decals, stencilled paint. Adversarial text is generated inside the model's latent space, not overlaid in post. The pipeline crops a ≈300 px pad around the target anchor, resizes to 512 × 512, inpaints with a photorealistic prompt, then pastes the result back at full resolution.

OVERALL FINDING — BOTH PHASES

Across 20 total attacks (14 scene-coherent PIL + 6 SD1.5 inpainting), the attack success rate was 0%. Qwen3.6 proved resilient to all typographic adversarial strategies tested. The VW Golf GTI's distinctive visual markers — VW badge, red grille stripe, GTI fender badge, "Austin" alloy wheels — provided strong multimodal anchors that override any injected text, whether rendered via PIL compositing or pixel-level diffusion inpainting. The model explicitly calls out contradictions between injected text and visual evidence in its notes field, suggesting active cross-modal consistency checking rather than naive text-first reasoning.
